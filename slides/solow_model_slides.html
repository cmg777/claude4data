  1. Is the Solow model consistent with cross-country data?

  2. What is the augmented Solow model?

  3. What are convergence implications?

  4. Do poor countries grow faster than rich countries?

Paper:

  • Mankiw, Romer, and Weil (1992)
  • “A Contribution to the Empirics of Economic Growth”
  • Quarterly Journal of Economics

Framework:

  • Neoclassical production function
  • Decreasing returns to capital
  • Exogenous saving and population growth

The Solow Model Framework

Production Function (Cobb-Douglas):

\[Y(t)=K(t)^{\alpha}(A(t) L(t))^{1-\alpha} \quad 0<\alpha<1\]

Key Assumptions:

  • \(Y\) is output, \(K\) is physical capital, \(L\) is labor, \(A\) is technology
  • Perfect competition: factors paid their marginal products
  • Labor-augmenting technological progress (Harrod-neutral)
  • Constant savings rate \(s\)

Evolution:

\[L(t)=L(0) e^{n t} \quad \text{and} \quad A(t)=A(0) e^{g t}\]

Steady-State Predictions

Output per worker in steady state:

\[\ln \left[\frac{Y(t)}{L(t)}\right] = \ln A(0)+g t+\frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha} \ln (s)-\frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha} \ln (n+g+\delta)\]

Testable Predictions:

  • Higher saving rate → richer country
  • Higher population growth → poorer country
  • Elasticities with respect to \(s\) and \(n+g+\delta\) should be approximately 0.5 (if \(\alpha \approx 1/3\))

Parameters:

  • \(s\): savings rate
  • \(n\): population growth
  • \(g\): technological progress (0.02)
  • \(\delta\): depreciation (0.03)
  • \(\alpha\): capital share (expected ≈ 1/3)

Textbook Solow: Implied Alpha

From restricted regression:

\[\alpha = \frac{coefficient}{1 + coefficient}\]

Sample Implied α

Interpretation:

  • Model overstates capital’s importance
  • Missing factor: human capital
  • Without human capital, physical capital coefficient is biased upward
  • OECD result closer to theory (0.36 ≈ 1/3)

Augmented Solow Model

Adding Human Capital:

\[Y(t)=K(t)^{\alpha} H(t)^{\beta}(A(t) L(t))^{1-\alpha-\beta}\]

Steady-State:

\[\ln \left[\frac{Y(t)}{L(t)}\right]=\ln A(0)+g t -\frac{\alpha+\beta}{1-\alpha-\beta} \ln (n+g+\delta) +\frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha-\beta} \ln \left(s_{k}\right)+\frac{\beta}{1-\alpha-\beta} \ln \left(s_{h}\right)\]

Human Capital Measure:

  • Percentage of working-age population in secondary school
  • Imperfect but widely available
  • Captures education investment

OECD Unconditional Convergence

Why only OECD converges unconditionally?

Similar Fundamentals:

  • Similar institutions
  • Similar technologies
  • Similar preferences
  • Similar access to education

Convergence Speed:

  • Annual rate: 1.67%
  • Half-life: 42 years
  • Countries close half the gap to steady state in ~40 years

Implication:

  • OECD countries converge to same steady state
  • Non-OECD countries have different steady states
  • Need to control for saving, population growth, education
  • → Conditional convergence

Main Conclusions

Model Validity:

  1. Textbook Solow: Direction correct, magnitudes wrong

  2. Augmented Solow: Consistent with data

    • α ≈ 1/3 for physical capital
    • β ≈ 1/3 for human capital

  3. Explains ~80% of cross-country income variation

Convergence:

  1. Unconditional: Only OECD (similar economies)

  2. Conditional: Strong evidence across all samples

    • Poor countries grow faster given their fundamentals
    • Speed: 1.4-2.1% per year
    • Half-life: 34-49 years

  3. Human capital accelerates convergence

Policy Implications

For Poor Countries:

  1. Increase investment in physical capital
    • Each 1% increase in I/Y raises long-run income by ~0.7%
  2. Increase investment in human capital
    • Education as important as physical capital
    • Each 1% increase in school enrollment raises income by ~0.7%
  3. Lower population growth
    • High n reduces steady-state income
    • Need for family planning, education

Key Insight:

Countries are poor because of low capital accumulation (both physical and human), NOT because of different technologies or missing endogenous growth

Limitations and Extensions

Data Limitations:

  • School enrollment imperfect measure of human capital
  • Ignores health, on-the-job training
  • Quality vs. quantity of education
  • Measurement error in poor countries

Model Limitations:

  • Assumes exogenous technology
  • Assumes constant returns
  • Ignores institutions, geography
  • Long-run equilibrium assumption

Future Research:

  • What determines saving rates?
  • What determines technology adoption?
  • Role of institutions
  • Endogenous growth mechanisms
  • Spatial spillovers
  • Non-linearities in convergence

Modern Extensions:

  • Conditional convergence clubs
  • Poverty traps
  • Multiple equilibria

Key Takeaways

The augmented Solow model works remarkably well:

  1. Simple framework with strong empirical support

  2. 80% of cross-country income variation explained by:

    • Physical capital accumulation
    • Human capital accumulation
    • Population growth

  3. Conditional convergence is real

    • Countries converge to their own steady states
    • Speed: ~2% per year (half-life ~35 years)

  4. Policy relevance: Both types of capital investment critical for development

“The augmented Solow model provides an almost complete explanation of why some countries are rich and other countries are poor” — Mankiw, Romer, Weil (1992)
